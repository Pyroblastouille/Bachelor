    How to Use the Principle of Superposition to Determine the Magnitude of a Magnetic Field at Some Point Near Several Long Straight Current-Carrying Wires

    Step 1: Use the formula {eq}B=\frac{\mu _{0}I}{2\pi r} {/eq} to find the magnitude of the magnetic field due to each wire at the point in question.

    Step 2: Apply the right hand rule to determine the direction of the magnetic field due to each wire at the point in question.

    Step 3: Find the sum of all the magnetic fields at the point in question.

    What is the Principle of Superposition?

    Superposition of Magnetic Fields: The total magnetic field at any point in space can be the result of multiple overlapping fields that add to each other according to vector mathematics.

    For example, if a point in space has a magnetic field of 10 T directed up and another field of 10 T pointing down the net field at that point is zero. If both fields were pointing up the net field at that point in space would be 20 T. In both cases the net field was calculated as the vector sum of the individual fields.

    Magnetic Field due to Long Straight Wires: When several current carrying wires are in a region near to each other they will all contribute to the total magnetic field at any point in the vicinity of the wires. To calculate the individual contributions to the magnetic field we can use the equation

    $$B=\frac{\mu _{0}I}{2\pi r} $$

    where {eq}B {/eq} is the strength of the magnetic field in tesla (T), {eq}I {/eq} is current in amperes (A), {eq}r {/eq} is the distance from the wire in meters (m), and {eq}\mu _{0} {/eq} is {eq}4\pi \times 10^{-7}\:Tm/A {/eq}.

    So, let's try using the steps above to determine the magnitude of a magnetic field in the following two examples!

    Examples of Using the Principle of Superposition to Determine the Magnitude of a Magnetic Field at Some Point Near Several Long Straight Current-Carrying Wires

    Example 1

    The image below depicts two long straight current carrying wires. Determine the magnetic field at a point 1 meter to the left of the wires.

    Example 1

    Step 1: Use the equation {eq}B=\frac{\mu _{0}I}{2\pi r} {/eq} to find the magnitude of the magnetic field due to each wire at the point in question.

    The wire on the left is 1 meter from the point:

    $$B_{1}=\frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}\:Tm/A)(2\:A)}{2\pi (1\:m)}=4.0\times 10^{-7}\:T $$

    The wire on the right is 1.5 meters from the point:

    $$B_{2}=\frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}\:Tm/A)(1\:A)}{2\pi (1.5\:m)}=1.33\times 10^{-7}\:T $$

    Step 2: Apply the right hand rule to determine the direction of the magnetic field due to each wire at the point in question.

    With the thumb pointed in the direction of the current and the fingers curled around the wire the right hand rule indicates that the magnetic fields from both wires are directed out of the page. Since both fields point the same direction they will be added.

    Step 3: Find the sum of all the magnetic fields at the point in question.

    Adding the fields give us {eq}B=4.0\times 10^{-7}\:T + 1.33\times 10^{-7}\:T=5.33\times 10^{-7}\:T {/eq} directed out of the page.

    Example 2

    The image below depicts two long straight current carrying wires. Determine the magnetic field at a point halfway between the wires.

    Example 2

    Step 1: Use the equation {eq}B=\frac{\mu _{0}I}{2\pi r} {/eq} to find the magnitude of the magnetic field due to each wire at the point in question.

    The wire on the left is 0.25 meters from the point:

    $$B_{1}=\frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}\:Tm/A)(2\:A)}{2\pi (0.25\:m)}=1.6\times 10^{-6}\:T $$

    The wire on the right is 0.25 meters from the point:

    $$B_{2}=\frac{(4\pi \times 10^{-7}\:Tm/A)(1\:A)}{2\pi (0.25\:m)}=8.0\times 10^{-7}\:T $$

    Step 2: Apply the right hand rule to determine the direction of the magnetic field due to each wire at the point in question.

    With the thumb pointed in the direction of the current and the fingers curled around the wire the right hand rule indicates that the magnetic field from the wire on the left is into the page while the field due to the wire on the right is out of the page. Since they are oppositely directed we will subtract the fields.

    Step 3: Find the sum of all the magnetic fields at the point in question.

    Subtracting the fields give us {eq}B=1.6\times 10^{-6}\:T - 8.0\times 10^{-7}\:T=8.0\times 10^{-7}\:T {/eq} directed into the page.
